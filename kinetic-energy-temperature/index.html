Kinetic Energy Vs. Temperature





Kinetic energy. All moving objects (including atoms and molecules) have a form of energy called kinetic energy. This is the energy that objects have that is due to their movement. Because there are different types of motion, there are different kinds of kinetic energy. Two types of kinetic energy are (a) rotational kinetic energy and (b) translational kinetic energy. In this section, we will talk about the translational kinetic energy of an object. This is the kinetic energy of objects due to their movement over a distance. 

Translational kinetic energy is typically the type of kinetic energy people mean when they talk about "kinetic energy." Translational kinetic energy is also the type of kinetic energy that defines an object's temperature. In this section, we will be talking about translational kinetic energy, but we will refer to it as simply "kinetic energy" here.

The relationship between the kinetic energy of an object and its mass (m) and speed (or velocity, v) is shown below:


  • For objects moving at the same speed, more massive objects have greater kinetic energy than less massive objects. 
  • For objects of the same mass, faster-moving objects have greater kinetic energy than slower-moving objects.

Temperature. The temperature of an object is a measure of the average (translational!) kinetic energy (or energy of motion) of the particles (atoms and/or molecules) that make up that object. The kinetic energy of individual particles is determined by both their speed and mass. 

Let's say there are two objects made of the same types of particles (e.g., aluminum atoms). As shown in the picture below (a microscopic view), the aluminum atoms of Object 1 (below, left) are moving at about twice the average speed of the aluminum atoms of Object 2 (below, right). The length of the arrows represents the speed of the atoms: longer arrows mean higher speed. The direction of the arrows indicates the direction of motion at that time point.

                                                                                    
Note: Because these atoms make up a solid object, they do not move freely around, but rather vibrate in a fixed location.

  • Because its atoms are moving faster, the temperature of Object 1 is higher than the temperature of Object 2.

The temperature of an object also depends on the mass of its particles: the atoms and/or molecules that make up that object. 

Now let's say we have an aluminum object and a silver object. The aluminum atoms of Object 1 (shown in the picture below, left) and the silver atoms of Object 3 (shown below, right) have the same average speed.


  • A silver atom has more mass than an aluminum atom (see the Periodic table, which includes the "atomic mass" for each element). So, the atoms of the silver object (Object 3 above) have more average kinetic energy than the atoms of the aluminum object (Object 1 above). In other words, the silver object has a higher temperature than the aluminum object.
