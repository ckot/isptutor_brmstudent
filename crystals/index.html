Crystals

What are Crystals? When you think of crystals, you may think of pretty gems that are used in jewelry, like diamonds (April birthstone), amethysts (February birthstone), and peridot (August birthstone). Some different types of crystals are shown below. Maybe you can identify them.


Other examples of crystals include table salt (NaCl), ice (H2O in its solid form), and sugar (C12H22O11). But what makes something a crystal? Crystals are substances whose molecules are arranged in a repeating pattern. It is this repeated molecule arrangement that gives crystals qualities like shininess and colors that makes them look pretty to us.
  • For example, table salt is a crystal, made of a repeating pattern of NaCl molecules. The molecules are arranged like in the picture below. As you can see, the repeated pattern is very simple.

Q1 (basic understanding): Why do NaCl molecules form this pattern in salt crystals? (There may be more than one correct answer.)
(a) The Na sides of the NaCl molecules and the Cl sides of other NaCl molecules attract each other.

(b) The Na sides of the NaCl molecules and the Cl sides of other NaCl molecules repel each other.

(c) The Na sides of the NaCl molecules and the Na sides of other NaCl molecules repel each other.

(d) The Cl sides of the NaCl molecules and the Cl sides of other NaCl molecules repel each other.

Right! Both attractive forces from oppositely-charged sides of the NaCl crystal (Na-Cl and Cl-Na) and repelling forces from same-charged sides of the crystal (Na-Na and Cl-Cl) cause the alternating pattern found in salt crystals. So, (a), (c), and (d) are the correct responses.

  • Ice (the solid form of water, or H2O) is another example of a crystal, where H2O molecules are arranged in repeating patterns. There are many types of patterns that H2O molecules in ice can form. Two possible patterns are shown below. In both patterns, notice that the hydrogen atoms (smaller) in general are closer to oxygen atoms (larger) than other hydrogen atoms. And the oxygen atoms are closer to hydrogen than other oxygen atoms. (Some oxygen atoms look like they are closest to other oxygen atoms, but that's because it's hard to show 3 dimensions in a 2 dimensional picture, and the hydrogen atoms may be hidden by oxygen atoms that are in front of them.)



















The pictures above are "ball and stick" representations of ice crystals: regular patterns of H2O molecules. Oxygen atoms are shown as darker blue balls, hydrogen atoms are shown as lighter blue balls.

The bonds between atoms are black lines (solid to the left and dashed to the right). Remember that bonds are not physical "things", like atoms, and just represent attractive forces between certain atoms.



Q2: Why do you think oxygen atoms tend to be closer to hydrogen atoms than to other oxygen atoms in ice crystals? (For help answering this question, please see H2O in Molecules unit.)
This is due to the electric force. Oxygen atoms are closer to hydrogen than other oxygen atoms in ice crystals because the oxygen sides of the H2O molecules, which are negatively charged, are attracted to hydrogen sides of other molecules, which are positively charged.
