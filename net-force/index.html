Net Force



How quickly an object changes speed, or accelerates, in a given direction depends on the “net” force acting on the object in that direction. 

Net force. The “net” force in a given direction is the sum of all forces acting in that direction minus the sum of all forces acting in the opposite direction.  

In other words, equal forces on an object that are in opposite directions cancel each other out and do not affect the object's motion. Only the "left over" force in that direction will affect the object's acceleration in that direction. This "left over" force is the net force.


In the example above, the 15 N forces acting on the object to the right and to the left cancel each other. The "left over" 2 N acting on the object to the left is the net force acting on the object!


Another example of a "net" quantity. In general, the "net" of something is the total amount in one direction minus the total amount in the opposite direction. 

Another example is the "net" electric charge of atoms or molecules. This is the number of (positively-charged) protons minus the number of (negatively-charged) electrons. So, if an atom has 5 protons and 7 electrons, the net charge would be -2.
