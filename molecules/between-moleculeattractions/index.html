Between-Molecule Attractions






Why objects exist. For objects that are big enough for humans to see to exist, there must be forces that bond molecules to other molecules. The individual molecules of a particular substance (for example, salt, sugar, or water) themselves are attracted to other molecules of the same kind to form that substance. This is why we can see a drop of water, or a grain of salt or sugar.

Oppositely-charged areas of different molecules are attracted to each other due to electric forces. 
  • For example, individual NaCl (table salt) molecules are attracted to each other through electric forces from oppositely-charged sides of molecules. The negative (Cl) sides of the molecules and positive (Na) sides of the molecule attract each other.

  • Individual water molecules attract each other because the areas of the water molecules (H2O) that are oppositely charged attract each other. In the picture below, three water molecules are shown. The negatively charged parts of molecules (represented by red) attract the positively charged parts of other molecules (represented as blue). And vice versa. The attractions between water molecules are why water forms droplets that are large enough that you can see! If there were no attractions between H2O molecules, they would all separate from each other (and you wouldn't be able to see them at all because molecules are way too tiny to see with your eyes).




Sugar molecules (Advanced): Individual sugar molecules--such as sucrose--are attracted to each other. These attractions are caused by electric forces due to oppositely-charged areas of different sugar molecules. That is, areas of a sucrose molecule that are negatively charged are attracted to positively charged areas of another sucrose molecule (and vice versa).

Sugars are much more complex molecules than salts (such as NaCl, which is made of only two atoms). Because of this, the electric force attractions that hold individual sugar molecules together are harder to show.
