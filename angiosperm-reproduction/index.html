Angiosperm (Flowered Plant) Reproduction

Angiosperms are flowered plants. They are very important to humans and other animals. They are our sources of food, clothing, and medicine. Plants like cotton, sunflowers, vegetables, fruit, as well as trees are all examples of important angiosperms. Because of their importance, humans need to understand how angiosperms reproduce (so we can help them to reproduce, if necessary).

The picture below (left) shows a flower of an angiosperm plant. People often just think of the pretty petals as the “flower.” But, the flower is more than just its pretty petals! The flower also includes the reproductive parts of angiosperms. The pretty petals help attract insects to the flower, which helps the plants reproduce.



Reproductive parts of Angiosperms: The flower is the reproductive organ of the Angiosperm. The petals are colorful and attract things like insects (e.g., bees) and birds to help in the fertilization process. Some flowers have both male and female reproductive parts in them. Some flowers are either male or female. This flower shown above has both male and female reproductive parts.
  • Pistils (female). The entire female parts of the plant’s flower are the pistils, usually found in the center of the flower (as in the picture above). Pistils include three parts: a stigma, style, and an ovary. The stigma is the sticky tip of the pistil. The style is a slender stalk that connects the stigma to a hollow structure at the base of the flower called the ovary; this is the area that holds the ovule and within the ovule, one or more (very tiny) egg cells.
  • Stamens (male). The entire male parts of the plant’s flower are stamens, which are the thin stalks topped with small knobs at the top. The thin stalk is called the filament. The small knobs at the top are called anthers, which contain the flower’s pollen. Pollen is a sticky substance that is often yellow, orange, or white (in the picture above, you can see pollen grains that are sticking to the bee). Each individual pollen grain contains sperm cells.

Angiosperm reproduction: The process of reproduction of angiosperm plants begins with pollination. Pollination happens when pollen is moved from the anther (a male organ of the flower) to the stigma (a female organ of the flower). The stigma can be on either the same or a different flower that the pollen came from.

When a pollen grain reaches the stigma, it produces a pollen tube that grows down through the style to the ovary. In other words, the pollen grain “tunnels” down the style and to the ovary. This is shown in the picture below.



The sperm cells from the pollen and egg cell in the ovule (which is within the ovary) join together in the flower’s ovary to form a zygote (a fertilized egg cell). This is fertilization. The zygote is the beginning of a new plant inside the seed. The zygote develops into the embryo. The wall of the ovule becomes the seed coat, and the ovary becomes the fruit surrounding the seed.










The picture below shows a cross-sectional view of an ovary (light green) with an ovule inside (darker green) the ovary. This is shown at two points in time. At Time 1, the egg cell within the ovule has just been fertilized by pollen. The fertilized egg cell is magnified in this picture so you can see it. Since it's only a single cell, in reality, you could not see it with just your eyes. And Time 2: this is a later time, after the egg cell has started developing into a little plant embryo. (The ovary has also become larger, turning into an edible fruit.)


After the egg is fertilized, the ovule wall (yellow-orange in the picture above) hardens to become a seed. Inside the ovule-then-seed, the fertilized egg cell (zygote) develops into a plant embryo. The ovary, which surrounds the ovule-then-seed, also grows and becomes what we call the "fruit" we eat.   

In many angiosperms, there are actually several ovules within the ovary. Each ovule forms into a seed. The ovary (the light-green part of the pictures above) grows and becomes what we call the "fruit" and eat. The ovary pictured above kind of looks like a pear. We generally remove or avoid eating the seeds within the ovary/fruit.

Reproduction in Sunflowers

Parts of Sunflower flowers. In sunflowers (shown in the picture below), the disk florets are the darker parts in the middle of the flowers. (The disk florets form the shape of a disc, like a bagel or doughnut.) This is where sunflower seeds are produced. The disk florets are surrounded by bright yellow "ray florets", which look like petals. But each ray florets (or "petal") is actually a flower. But the petal-like ray florets cannot produce seeds; the ray florets are sterile.



Each disk floret (the darker yellow or orange parts that together form a donut shape) is a flower. So, what we call a sunflower is actually a whole bunch of little flowers! (Kind of crazy, right?)











Each disk floret has both male parts and female parts. The male parts of each floret include anthers, which have pollen grains that stick to it. You can see the anthers of the disk florets in the picture below. The female parts of each floret are the stigma, the style (hidden behind the anthers in the picture below), and the ovary. Pollination happens when pollen somehow gets on the surfaces of the stigma. One way that pollen gets from anthers to the stigma is when it sticks to insects like bees. Bees might pick up pollen on the hairs of their bodies when they walk on the flowers or even when they just fly by the flowers (see the unit: Angiosperm Pollinators (Bees) for how this happens!). Bees and other insects can then transfer the pollen they have picked up to the stigma and other parts of the same or a different flower when they walk around feeding on the sunflowers.


Pollination is the first step in creating seeds. When pollen gets on the stigma, it starts tunneling down the stigma until it gets to the ovary. The sperm cells from the pollen and egg cell in the ovule join together in the flower’s ovary to form a zygote (a fertilized egg cell). This eventually forms a sunflower seed.

     


Sunflower seeds. The seeds of the sunflower plant, which is also an angiosperm, are a bit different from other types of seeds. What people tend to think of as a sunflower "seed" (the black and white covering, shown below, to the left) is actually the "fruit" of the sunflower from the ovary (even though you wouldn't want to eat it!). The seed of the sunflower (shown below, right) is actually inside the hard fruit exterior. The seed developed from the sunflower's fertilized ovule. This is what people eat as snacks.
