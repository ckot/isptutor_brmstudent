Plants




What are Plants? Plants are living organisms composed of one or more cells. Like animal cells, each plant cell has a nucleus
 (see the picture of a plant cell to the right). 

One difference between plant and animal cells is that the cells of plants have "walls", which are rigid and firm. Animal cells do not have cell walls but thinner cell membranes. This makes sense because plants do not have skeletons that support them. If plant cells did not have firm cell walls, plants would be more or less shapeless blobs.

Plants usually grow in one location (rather than move around, like most animals do). Because of this, plants must depend on their local environment for nourishment.

Plants get some nutrients they need through the absorption of water and minerals through their roots.

Scientifically, we say plants are organisms that are autotrophic (auto means "self" and trophic means "nutrition" in Greek). As (I hope--if you are paying attention!) you figured out, this means that they produce their own food.

Plants make their own food (sugars) through the process of photosynthesis. In this process, sunlight provides the energy to convert carbon dioxide and water into sugars. This process happens in the chloroplasts in the cells of plant leaves (shown in the picture of a plant cell to the right).

What are plants made of? Most people would be surprised to learn that plants are mostly made from carbon dioxide (CO2), which comes from the air!!  This is why plants (including trees) are so important to our environment: they remove CO2 (a gas that absorbs energy from sunlight) from the air. This helps keep the Earth from getting too warm.





Plants can be divided into two main groups: Vascular and Non-vascular. (See the diagram below.)
  • Vascular plants have cells that form tubes for carrying water and nutrients throughout the plant. Non-vascular plants do not have tubes to carry water and nutrients. Instead, water and nutrients move from cells to their neighboring cells. (In case you were wondering, this is why (as shown in the picture of the plant cell above) the cell walls have little holes in them. This is so water and other nutrients can move from cell to cell!)
